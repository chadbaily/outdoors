Why do you want to be an officer? I frequently hear and think of ways in which the Outdoors Club can be expanded or improved. I hope to fill niches in the club that are not being currently explored, so I would constantly seek to ask members what trips they would like to see led. For example, few environmental service, horseback riding, or sailing trips were led in the past year. I have plans to lead all three of these trips within the semester. I would also like to create more opportunities for members of the club to interact. I felt like last year it was difficult to meet members of the club who favored different types of outdoor activities. I strongly believe that we should have regular outdoor movie nights, Potlucks, and spring trips to Blue Hole to hike and swim. I would like to advise younger members, not just on how to lead trips, but also on what sizes and brands of gear will suit them best, where they should travel, or even what outdoors magazines are best to subscribe to (Backpacker is my love). I am vivacious and friendly, so new members would find me approachable. I am ridiculously organized, often to the point of being referred to as OCD. I would like to be an officer because I want to have opportunities to share my love of the outdoors with others, helping them to become just as passionate about it as I am. I have helped at least five people register this semester, and hearing their stories from the great times they had hiking or backpacking was extremely rewarding. Ever since a hike last year in which my group became lost in the woods, and our leader followed an old hunting path to find the top of the mountain, I have been inspired to find new ways to interpret maps and to explore off the beaten path. Last year Brian Tai taught me that I was a better skier than I realized by literally pushing me down a black diamond in five foot visibility. He as a leader showed me how to find my own strength. At the kayaking roll workshop last year, I was trapped under kayak for two minutes, and terrified out of my mind, but the officer convinced me that I had nothing to fear, and talked me into immediately trying again. After that, I had the confidence to travel on a three day kayaking trip to the Eastern Shore. I want to be the person who refuses to loosen the top rope until someone has the confidence to climb to the top of the wall (this has also happened to me: I was left dangling fifty feet into the air, until I agreed, yes, I actually can finish this course). I want to teach them to push their limits and have faith in themselves, while at the same time teaching them to appreciate the beauty of the outdoors.
